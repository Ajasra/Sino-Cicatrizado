# **Ouro Preto: Confluences of Colonial Memory, Decolonial Cybernetics, and Systemic Resistance**

## **The Cybernetic Topography of a World Heritage Site**

Ouro Preto, positioned within the rugged, mountainous topography of the Serra do Espinhaço in Minas Gerais, Brazil, functions as a highly complex, dynamic system rather than a static repository of eighteenth-century colonial architecture. The city represents an environment where overlapping temporalities—layers of extreme historical wealth, brutal colonial extraction, Afro-diasporic resistance, and contemporary technological integration—continuously interact and modulate one another. As the designated site for the August 2026 American Society for Cybernetics (ASC) Conference, themed "Conversational Confluences," the municipality provides a uniquely charged geographical and cultural laboratory. It demands an exploration of the intersections between systems theory, decolonial thought, and participatory art, setting the stage for projects that interrogate how historical memory informs future technological and social viability.  
To engage with Ouro Preto through a cybernetic lens requires analyzing the city as an ongoing feedback loop of material and immaterial forces. Its physical infrastructure, characterized by steep cobblestone streets, expansive subterranean gold mines, and monumental Rococo churches, operates as the hardware upon which centuries of social, cultural, and political software have been continuously encoded and overwritten. A comprehensive examination of Ouro Preto’s colonial history, its cultural mechanisms of resistance, its self-organizing social structures, and the emerging paradigms of decolonial cybernetics establishes the necessary foundation for conceptualizing profound, site-specific interventions.

## **The Crucible of Extraction: Colonial History and the Gold Cycle**

### **The Architecture of the Gold Rush and Open-Loop Extraction**

The foundational history of Ouro Preto is intrinsically linked to an open-loop system of resource extraction initiated by the Portuguese Empire. The settlement emerged following the discovery of gold in the Tripuí riverbed in the late seventeenth century, officially incorporating as Vila Rica (Rich Village) in 1711\. By the mid-eighteenth century, driven by the insatiable demands of the gold rush, Vila Rica had exploded into an unprecedented metropolitan center with approximately 110,000 inhabitants. This demographic density rendered it the largest city in the Americas at the time, functioning at twice the population scale of contemporary New York City.  
This demographic and economic explosion transformed the region into the world's primary supplier of gold, producing over 70% of the global supply during the eighteenth century. It is estimated that approximately 800 tons of gold were extracted and funneled to Portugal, a massive wealth transfer that fundamentally altered the global economic equilibrium. This capital injection filled European coffers, financed the construction of monumental palaces in both Portugal and Brazil, and crucially, serviced Portuguese debts to British money lenders in London, thereby fueling broader European industrial expansion.  
However, this staggering wealth accumulation was built entirely upon the violent subjugation and forced labor of enslaved African populations. The vast riches extracted from the earth created a society characterized by extreme stratification and systemic cruelty. The physical environment of Ouro Preto remains a striking testament to this era, defined by opulent Baroque and Rococo churches adorned with hundreds of kilograms of gold, standing in stark, structural opposition to the subterranean labyrinths where enslaved populations endured harrowing, claustrophobic conditions to extract the very minerals that decorated the world above. The Estrada Real (Royal Road) was constructed by enslaved labor specifically to optimize the logistical transport of these minerals to the coastal ports of Rio de Janeiro and Paraty, serving as a heavily guarded conduit for imperial wealth.

### **The Inconfidência Mineira: Early Feedback against Imperial Control**

By the late eighteenth century, as the easily accessible alluvial gold reserves began to dwindle, the Portuguese Crown sought to maintain its revenue streams through increasingly aggressive and rigid taxation protocols. This external control mechanism generated intense systemic friction within the colonial apparatus, culminating in the *Inconfidência Mineira* (Minas Gerais Conspiracy) of 1789\. Inspired by the success of the American Revolution against the British crown and the circulation of Enlightenment ideals—despite severe Portuguese censorship on printed media—local intellectuals, military figures, and poets, such as Tomás Antônio Gonzaga, formed a separatist movement intended to sever ties with Portugal and declare an independent Brazilian republic.  
The conspiracy, functioning as an early negative feedback loop attempting to regulate imperial extraction, was ultimately poorly organized and swiftly crushed by Portuguese military forces. Its most prominent leader, Joaquim José da Silva Xavier, known universally as Tiradentes (the "tooth puller"), was hanged, drawn, and quartered on April 21, 1792\. His body parts were displayed across the region to serve as a brutal, visceral deterrent against future systemic decoupling from colonial rule. Today, Tiradentes is revered as the ultimate martyr of Brazilian independence, and the Praça Tiradentes—the central square in Ouro Preto where his head was reportedly exhibited—houses the Museu da Inconfidência. This museum, occupying the magnificent former town hall and prison constructed between 1785 and the nineteenth century, serves as the central archival node preserving the memory of this libertarian ideal.

## **Materializing the Sacred: Aleijadinho and the Brazilian Baroque**

### **The Synthesis of Form, Identity, and Constraint**

The immense, unprecedented wealth of the gold cycle attracted European intellectuals, artisans, and religious orders, transforming Ouro Preto into a highly sophisticated hub of artistic innovation. The defining aesthetic output of this era is the Mineiro Rococo, a regional, hybridized adaptation of Baroque architecture that traded the rigid, classical lines of European tradition for undulating, dynamic curves, elliptical floor plans, and highly irregular ornamentation. The undisputed master of this movement, and a central figure in Brazilian art history, was Antônio Francisco Lisboa, universally known as *Aleijadinho* (the "Little Cripple").  
Born around 1738 in Vila Rica as the illegitimate son of Portuguese architect Manoel Francisco Lisboa and an enslaved African woman, Aleijadinho embodies the syncretic and highly contradictory nature of colonial Brazilian society. Despite his clear genius, the racist structural parameters of colonial society excluded him from prestigious white religious fraternities, forcing him to join a mulatto brotherhood. He frequently operated as a day laborer rather than a master architect, while white contractors secured the official commissions and took credit for his designs. In his late thirties, he contracted a severe, degenerative disease that slowly destroyed his fingers and toes, leading to intense physical deformity and profound social isolation.  
Aleijadinho’s later career represents an extraordinary adaptation to compounding physical and social constraints. Stripped of his extremities, he reportedly had his assistants strap chisels and hammers directly to his wrists, continuing to sculpt intricate details in cedar wood and local soapstone (*pedra de sabão*). His works, defined by exquisite tension, robust volume, and emotional intensity, became the foundational visual language of Brazilian identity. Modernist architect Lúcio Costa, while cataloging Aleijadinho's work, observed that his architecture was marked by an "ailing sensuousness" and that he possessed the spirit of a decorator who lost himself in details, inadvertently projecting a "tortured and repressed soul" into the stone. Yet, despite Costa's critiques of his structural cohesion, Aleijadinho is almost universally recognized as the greatest exponent of colonial art in the Americas.

### 

### **Architectural Masterpieces and Spatial Dynamics**

Aleijadinho’s legacy is scattered across Ouro Preto and neighboring towns such as Mariana, Sabará, and São João del-Rei. His undisputed architectural masterpiece in Ouro Preto is the Church of São Francisco de Assis (1766–1794). The church features a groundbreaking, highly rhythmic design: the facade relies on concavely curved lateral bays that end in cylindrical bell towers, offsetting the straight lines of Portuguese tradition. The plan utilizes golden rectangles and complex spatial transitions, while the exterior soapstone medallion, carved by Aleijadinho, depicts St. Francis receiving the stigmata.  
Further afield in Congonhas do Campo, Aleijadinho executed the Sanctuary of Bom Jesus de Matosinhos, a pilgrimage complex designed to emulate the ascent to Mount Calvary. Here, he carved 64 life-sized wooden figures representing the Passion of Christ, housed in small chapels along a zigzagging path. At the summit, large, exquisitely detailed sculptures of the Twelve Prophets, carved in soapstone, line the parapets of the exterior staircase. These prophets interact dynamically with the landscape and the viewer, creating shifting compositional arrangements that prefigure interactive, spatial design, prompting art historians to compare the ensemble to a tremendous, portentous ballet.

## **Afro-Brazilian Agency: The Viable System of Chico Rei**

While the prevailing historical narrative of Ouro Preto often centers on the opulence of the Portuguese Crown, the political machinations of the Inconfidência, and the artistic genius of Aleijadinho, the actual wealth of the city was physically extracted by enslaved Africans. Amidst this oppressive, life-destroying regime, instances of profound Afro-diasporic resistance and structural subversion emerged. The most famous and enduring of these narratives is the legend of Chico Rei (Galanga).

### 

### **The Encardideira Mine and Strategic Emancipation**

According to deeply rooted oral tradition, Galanga was a monarch in the Kingdom of Kongo before being captured, surviving the horrors of the Middle Passage, and being sold into slavery in Vila Rica around 1740, along with members of his family and tribe. Recognized as a leader by his enslaved compatriots and the Portuguese slave traders alike, he was given the moniker "Chico Rei" (Little King). Assigned to work in the subterranean gold mines, Chico Rei orchestrated a slow, covert accumulation of capital by hiding gold dust in his hair, washing it out at night to steadily build a hidden reserve.  
Over years of arduous labor, Chico Rei amassed sufficient gold to purchase his own freedom, followed by the freedom of his son and eventually his entire community. Demonstrating extraordinary strategic acumen and an understanding of the colonial economic apparatus, he subsequently purchased the very mine where he had been enslaved—the Encardideira Mine, now famously known as the Mina do Chico Rei. Under his ownership, the mine flourished, and he utilized the profits to buy the freedom of many other enslaved Black people in the town, establishing himself as a wealthy, respected free Black mine owner and community leader.

### 

### **The Brotherhood of Our Lady of the Rosary**

With the ongoing profits from the mine, Chico Rei and his newly emancipated community funded the construction of the Church of Santa Efigênia, also known as the Church of Our Lady of the Rosary of the Blacks (*Igreja de Nossa Senhora do Rosário dos Pretos*). Black Catholic brotherhoods (*irmandades*) operated as critical mutual aid societies in colonial Brazil. While superficially conforming to colonial religious structures, these brotherhoods provided a socially acceptable cover for the preservation of African cultural practices, communal organizing, and the pooling of resources to purchase manumission for other enslaved individuals.  
The architecture and iconography of these churches reflect this subversive agency, frequently featuring Black saints, such as Saint Iphigenia, and incorporating African aesthetic sensibilities into the Mineiro Rococo style. Though some academic historians debate the literal, documentary veracity of Chico Rei's existence due to a lack of primary written sources, noting that his tale likely amalgamates the experiences of several successful freedmen, his story remains a powerful, undeniable fixture in the collective memory of Ouro Preto. He functions as a profound symbol of resilience—a historical actor who successfully infiltrated the colonial economic system, decoded its mechanisms of capital, and utilized its own outputs to construct an autonomous, viable subsystem for his people. The Mina do Chico Rei, which stretches over 8 square kilometers with 175 open galleries, remains open to the public today, offering a visceral look into the claustrophobic reality of colonial extraction.

## **Sonic Cybernetics: The Language of the Bells**

One of the most fascinating systemic features of Ouro Preto is its acoustic environment. The city is renowned for its historic church bells, which function not merely as musical instruments, but as a sophisticated, city-wide communication network. This auditory code, known as the *Toque dos Sinos* (Ringing of the Bells) or *Linguagem dos Sinos* (Language of the Bells), was officially inscribed in the Book of Forms of Expression as intangible cultural heritage by the Brazilian Institute of National Historic and Artistic Heritage (IPHAN) in 2009\.

### 

### **An Auditory Information Network**

Long before the advent of the telegraph, radio, or digital communication, the bells of Ouro Preto and the wider Minas Gerais region organized the temporal, religious, and social fabric of the city. The system utilizes a complex vocabulary of "tolls" (when the bell swings to make slow, repeated sounds) and "peals" (when the bell ringer pulls the clapper to make rapid chimes) to transmit highly specific data across the mountainous topography.  
Different rhythmic sequences and tonal combinations broadcast discrete messages without the need for words: calling the faithful to mass, marking the passage of civil time, warning of fires or the invasion of enemy forces, or announcing the arrival of ecclesiastical authorities. Crucially, the bells convey nuanced demographic data regarding deaths in the community. Distinct chimes indicate whether the deceased is a man, a woman, a child, a priest, or a bishop. The older residents of the region remain highly adept at decoding this sonic information, effectively transforming the acoustic landscape into a shared informational matrix that binds the community.

### **Subversion and Polyrhythmic Syncopation**

The role of the *sineiro* (bell ringer) was historically hazardous, physically demanding labor, requiring the ringer to project their body outward from the parapet to rotate massive metal bells. This dangerous task was frequently assigned to enslaved Black men. While strictly expected to execute rigid European ecclesiastical scores, these enslaved sineiros engaged in a form of acoustic resistance. They utilized the remote bell towers as sites of artistic and cultural creation, infusing the traditional tolls with African percussion techniques, syncopated rhythms, and polyrhythmic flourishes.  
This synthesis created a unique, hybridized sonic language specific to the Minas Gerais region. Today, contemporary sineiros continue this deeply rooted tradition, imprinting their individual psychological states and musical training onto their performances. They strike multiple clappers simultaneously against various bells, generating complex rhythms reminiscent of Afro-Brazilian cultural expressions like capoeira and congado. As a research vector for cybernetic theory, the Language of the Bells represents an early, localized manifestation of an acoustic communication channel—one where the strict signaling protocols of the colonial church were hacked, modulated, and expanded by marginalized actors to transmit alternative cultural frequencies.

## **Autopoiesis and Autogestão: The Cybernetics of the UFOP Repúblicas**

Moving from the colonial era to the contemporary social fabric, Ouro Preto is defined by its massive university student population. The Federal University of Ouro Preto (UFOP), established in 1969 through the merger of the historic School of Pharmacy and the School of Mines (founded in 1876 by Dom Pedro II), hosts over 11,000 students and generates profound cultural and economic impacts. The university's presence has fostered a unique, highly resilient social infrastructure: the *repúblicas*.

### **The Architecture of Self-Management**

In stark contrast to traditional university dormitories overseen by centralized institutional bureaucracy, UFOP maintains a system of 59 "repúblicas federais"—historic properties located throughout the city center that are owned by the university but ceded entirely to student groups for absolute self-governance (*autogestão*). Each república operates as an autonomous social unit, responsible for its own internal regulations, financial maintenance, physical upkeep, and conflict resolution.  
This model of *autogestão* is structurally identical to a self-organizing cybernetic system, specifically aligning with the principles of autopoiesis. The residents share all general expenses (water, electricity, internet), manage daily operations through democratic direct action, and cultivate a distinct house identity. These identities are highly codified, often complete with unique anthems, extensive historical archives, and powerful alumni networks spanning decades, ensuring that the organism of the house survives long after individual students graduate.

### **The "Batalha" as Requisite Variety and Boundary Maintenance**

The most critical regulatory mechanism of the república system is the selection of new residents, a rigorous, months-long probationary period known as the *batalha* (the battle). When incoming freshmen (colloquially termed *bixos* or *bichos*) apply to live in a república, they undergo an extended evaluation—lasting anywhere from three months to a year—overseen by the senior residents (*decanos*). During the *batalha*, candidates are intensely assessed on their solidarity, sense of community, willingness to perform household maintenance tasks, and their ability to assimilate the house's specific traditions.  
While sociological studies and ex-residents frequently point out that the *batalha* can enforce rigid hierarchies, foster insular "social bubbles," and demand excessive, sometimes exploitative labor from new students, the internal logic of the repúblicas frames it as an essential filtering mechanism. From an organizational cybernetics standpoint, the *batalha* serves as the system's boundary-maintenance function. It processes external inputs (new students) and systematically calibrates them to the internal environment, dampening external fluctuations to ensure internal homeostasis and the long-term viability of the community structure.

## **Decolonial Cybernetics: Nêgo Bispo and the Theory of Confluence**

The convergence of systems theory, colonial history, and ecological awareness in Ouro Preto provides the direct conceptual scaffolding for the 2026 American Society for Cybernetics (ASC) conference, titled "Conversational Confluences". The theoretical anchor of the conference, and a vital key to designing relevant interventions in the city, is the philosophical framework established by the late Antônio Bispo dos Santos, known as Nêgo Bispo.

### **Cosmophobia vs. Biointeraction**

Nêgo Bispo was a prominent *quilombola* intellectual, poet, and political activist from the Sertão of Piauí, deeply embedded in the land rights struggles of traditional Afro-Brazilian and Indigenous (what he termed Afro-Pindoramic) communities. Translating his quilombola cosmovision into powerful literature, his writings—such as *A terra dá, a terra quer* (The land gives, the land wants)—formulate a profound epistemological critique of Western modernity.  
Bispo diagnosed the driving force of colonial, capitalist society as *cosmophobia*—a deep-seated fear of the cosmos and nature that drives human beings to artificially detach themselves from the natural world and seek to dominate it. Cosmophobic societies view life entirely as raw material to be extracted and synthesized into capital, a paradigm perfectly exemplified by the gold rush of Ouro Preto. In contrast, Bispo proposed *biointeraction* and *organic thought*, wherein humans recognize themselves as an integrated component of a polytheistic, multispecies ecology.

### **The Epistemology of Confluence**

Central to ASC 2026 is Bispo's concept of *confluence*. Bispo observed that in Western (monotheistic, colonial) epistemology, the meeting of two differing entities usually results in *transfluence*—assimilation, homogenization, expropriation, or the destruction of one by the other. Confluence, however, derives its metaphor from nature: when two rivers meet, neither ceases to be a river; instead, they join to become a stronger, more resilient body of water, mutually amplifying each other without erasing their distinct, intrinsic identities.  
This framework offers a radical rethinking of collaboration and systems interaction. It demands a move away from universalizing models of control toward "pluriversal" coexistence. Furthermore, Bispo emphasized the power of *naming* as a counter-colonial strategy, urging traditional peoples to engage in a "war of denominations" by reclaiming and resignifying the derogatory language imposed by colonizers, thereby weakening the colonial hold on reality and repossessing their own narratives. His philosophy extended deeply into generational memory; he viewed the relationship between generations not as linear, but as cyclical (the "beginning, the middle, and the beginning"), where the grandparents' generation prepares the grandchildren for the future, while simultaneously preparing themselves to become ancestors who live on as active, dialogic memory triggered by sensory inputs like sound and image.

## **Digital Heritage and Cybernetic Preservation**

As Ouro Preto moves deeper into the twenty-first century, its physical vulnerabilities demand technological intervention. Centuries after their construction, the city's monuments face existential threats from environmental degradation and fire, a risk tragically highlighted by the catastrophic blaze at the National Museum in Rio de Janeiro and a local fire at Mariana's Church of Our Lady of Mount Carmel, which destroyed priceless Rococo paintings.

### 

### **Ouro Preto Forever and HBIM**

To counteract systemic decay, the *Ouro Preto Forever* project has pioneered the use of Heritage Building Information Modeling (HBIM) to create highly accurate "digital twins" of 24 Baroque churches in the region. Using advanced terrestrial laser scanners (such as the Leica RTC360) and autonomous UAVs (Leica BLK2FLY), researchers capture the millimeter-level geometry of these structures, including the intricate wooden beam networks hidden in the roofs and narrow, dark spaces infested with spiders and bats.  
The resulting 3D point clouds are processed and combined with historical documentation to create rich data models. These digital twins serve multiple functions: they provide precise baseline data for rapid restoration in the event of a disaster, generate 3D-printed tactile replicas of churches and altars for blind visitors, facilitate Virtual Reality (VR) research for students, and are increasingly integrated with localized Internet of Things (IoT) sensor networks (fire alarms, security cameras) to enable real-time facility management by the resident priests and government officials.

### **Technological Art and the MARTE Festival**

Beyond mere preservation, technology in Ouro Preto is being utilized as a medium for active cultural dialogue. The city hosts the annual MARTE Festival (Music, Art, Technology, and Education), which explicitly explores the intersections between contemporary digital culture and ancestral heritage. With programming centered around the concept "The Future is Ancestral — and comes from the Global South," the festival features monumental laser mapping projections on the facade of the Museu da Inconfidência, performances by Indigenous and Afro-diasporic artists, and debates on artificial intelligence and algorithmic racism. Alongside the traditional Winter Festival (Festival de Inverno) run by UFOP and the municipal government, Ouro Preto is proving to be a fertile environment for technological experimentation.
