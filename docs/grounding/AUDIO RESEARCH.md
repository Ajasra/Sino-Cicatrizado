#### [1] [Spectralism | The Microtonal Piano](https://www.themicrotonalpiano.net/about-the-project/context/listening/spectralism/)

**Insights:**
- Spectral music, founded by Gérard Grisey and Tristan Murail in the early 1970s at IRCAM, uses FFT analysis of sound spectra as compositional material, focusing on manipulating and transforming timbral features derived from sonographic representations.
- The spectral approach treats sound as a phenomenological object to be explored—harmonies and orchestrations are built from harmonic and inharmonic partials of complex sounds like bells or bird song, often involving microtonal gradations (quarter-tones, eighth-tones) and non-metric durations.
- Grisey sought to blur the line between harmonic overtones and noise, and was fascinated by slow, all-parameter-affecting musical processes distinct from minimalism; Murail emphasizes spectralism as an aesthetic attitude (music is sound evolving in time) rather than a fixed style.
- A key motivation was to integrate electronic and acoustic sound seamlessly; influence came from Scelsi's meditations, Ligeti's sound clouds, and Xenakis's sonic architecture.

# Sonic Intra-Actions: Toward a Diffractive Foundation for Experimental Sound Art

## 0. Agential Cut Declaration
This synthesis focuses on the material-discursive entanglements that connect experimental music theory, live coding performance, and posthumanist sound aesthetics, as they bear directly on the design of intra-active sonic artworks. Included are perceptual frameworks (Wanke), spectral composition (Grisey, Murail), live coding platforms and their cultural politics (TidalCycles, SuperCollider, Strudel, Algorave, feminist/decolonial movements), glitch and error as semiotic-material agents (Betancourt, Oval, Ikeda), new materialist and agential-realist ontologies of sound (Cox, Cecchetto, Barad), and the technical affordances of real-time spectral processing (FFT UGens, Euclidean rhythm algorithms). Excluded are conventional Western music theory (tonal harmony, form), commercial music production paradigms, and any approach that treats sound as a carrier of pre-existing human meaning rather than as a vibrant, intra-active substance. The synthesis takes the form of a diffractive map—a constellation of interferences rather than a linear review—because the artwork it serves is itself an apparatus-in-becoming that demands not a fixed foundation but a set of charged, friction-laden conceptual and technical nodes.

## 1. Diffractive Interference

### Lens 1: Glitch as Agential Cut
Reading glitch theory through Barad’s agential realism [S3, S32, S33] and Oval’s/Ryoji Ikeda’s practices [S18, S22] yields an interference pattern where error is not a failure but the moment the apparatus speaks. Betancourt frames glitch as semiotic rupture [S3]; when diffracted through the agential cut, this rupture becomes the contingent boundary-making that resolves the ontological inseparability of coder, code, machine, and sound [S33]. The glitch is thus the scar of the cut—the audible trace of an exclusion. In live coding, the screen projection makes the algorithm visible, and any runtime error becomes a performative agential cut, exposing the apparatus’s non-neutrality [S7, S21]. This directly materializes the anti-interactionist principle that entanglement, not seamlessness, is the condition of experience. However, the semiotic emphasis of Betancourt resists full materialist dissolution: glitch as sign still assumes a human subject who reads it. Ikeda’s works, by converting raw data streams into sound and light, bypass semiosis and operate at the threshold of perception—a vibrational, not signifying, glitch [S18, S19]. The interference thus marks a productive tension: we can design for the glitch-as-voice (material) and the glitch-as-sign (discursive), letting them flicker indecidedly within the same installation.

### Lens 2: Vibrational Ontology Meets Euclidean Algorithm
Cox’s sonic flux [S27] proposes sound as a continuous material flow that precedes and exceeds human expression; Cecchetto’s humanesis [S30, S31] attends to the haunted gaps in posthumanist discourse that sound discloses. Diffracting these through the Euclidean rhythm algorithm [S16, S17, S48] illuminates how algorithmic structuring itself can be recast as an intra-active materialization of sonic flux. Bjorklund’s algorithm distributes onsets with maximal evenness—a physical distribution problem from nuclear engineering [S16] that, when mapped onto a musical pulse, produces patterns (tresillo, clave) found in diverse cultural traditions [S48]. This is not cultural appropriation but a resonance between material constraints (e.g., spacing, timing) and human rhythmic cognition, an instance of what Barad calls the entwinement of matter and meaning. In live coding (Tidal/Strudel), invoking `euclid(3,8)` is an agential cut that temporarily crystallizes a pattern from the flux of possible rhythms, and rotating it via `euclidRot` deterritorializes that cut into a nomadic variation [S47, S48]. Yet the algorithm’s mathematical purity seduces us into forgetting the cultural sediment it carries: the three‑against‑eight pattern is not only a mathematical relation but a rhythm of enslaved people’s resistance (tresillo). Diffraction demands we stage this interference—algorithm-as-apparatus and rhythm-as-colonial-archive—directly in the artwork, perhaps by coupling Euclidean sequences with field recordings of their cultural contexts, or by deliberately glitching the rotation pattern to expose the cut.

### Lens 3: Wanke’s Listening Modes through Spectral and Live Coding Practice
Wanke’s unified aesthetic framework identifies three modes of listening for sound-based music (not fully detailed in the sources, but implied as structural/timbral engagements) and a cross-genre “sound-in-itself” materialism [S2, S11, S46]. Diffracting these modes through spectral composition (Grisey, Murail) and live coding performance reveals a loop: spectralism already enacts a listening mode that treats sound as phenomenological object—an attention to partials, microtonal gradations, and slow timbral processes [S1]. Live coding, especially when it manipulates FFT in real time (PV_UGens, pvcollect) [S10, S14, S57], invites a new listening mode where the audience can see the code’s changes and hear the spectral reconfiguration simultaneously—an audiovisual intra‑activity. The missing three modes might correspond to: (1) timbral immersion (sound-as-environment), (2) structural expectation (algorithmic pattern recognition), and (3) glitch-attention (detection of error/rupture). This speculative trinity—though not confirmed—offers a design schema: an installation can orchestrate modulations among these modes, using visual code projection (live coding membrane) and FFT-driven sonic shifts to pull the participant from immersion to analytical tracking to startlement at a glitch. The fact that the concrete modes are absent [gap S2_modes] is itself a generative void that the artwork can sonically investigate: we can expose participants to spectral, Euclidean, and glitch textures and record their emergent listening strategies, retroactively perforating Wanke’s framework.

8. **[GAP_NON_TEXT_ARCHIVES]** Non‑textual and embodied sonic knowledges (oral traditions, field recordings, deaf/haptic listening) remain outside the source corpus, perpetuating a textual/archival bias.

These gaps are stable and assigned IDs for the next research pass, which will target full‑text retrieval, performance listening sessions, and hands‑on coding experiments to fill them.
